The Jagannath Temple in Puri is famous for its many mysteries and ancient traditions. It is believed that the temple flag always flies against the wind and the Sudarshan Chakra appears to face visitors from every direction. These fascinating beliefs, along with its sacred rituals, make the temple one of India's most remarkable pilgrimage sites.

History

The Jagannath Temple in Puri has a rich history that dates back to the 12th century. It was built by King Anantavarman Chodaganga Deva of the Eastern Ganga dynasty. The temple is dedicated to Lord Jagannath, a form of Lord Vishnu, and is one of the most important pilgrimage sites for Hindus. The temple's architecture is a blend of Kalinga and Dravidian styles, and it has been a center of religious and cultural activities for centuries.


Architecture

The temple is a magnificent example of Kalinga architecture with a towering main shrine and beautifully carved stone structures. The main temple rises to about 65 meters (214 feet) in height. Its impressive design and artistic carvings make it one of India's architectural masterpieces.


Ritual

Jagannath Temple follows a unique daily worship tradition with several rituals performed from early morning until night. The deities are bathed, dressed, offered food (Mahaprasad), and worshipped with prayers and devotional songs. The temple is especially famous for its grand Rath Yatra (Chariot Festival), where the deities are taken in beautifully decorated chariots through the streets of Puri.
